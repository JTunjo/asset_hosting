Saccharomyces Inoculation Protocol — 200 L (Coffee Cherry)

Scaled field protocol for infiltrating Saccharomyces spp. into intact coffee cherries

Objective

Enable internal colonization of coffee cherries by Saccharomyces spp. (e.g. S. cerevisiae K-97) using a field-adapted immersion/infiltration method. This protocol is optimized for a 200 L tank and avoids breaking the fruit skin while maximizing yeast establishment inside the mucilage.

1. Inoculant preparation (200 L)

  • Base (per L): boiled & cooled water 1 L; sugar/panela 20 g (2%); citric acid 0.5 g (≈pH 4.0–4.2).
  • Yeast load: target 1×106–1×107 cells/mL. For the full tank, prepare ~200–300 g active dry yeast, rehydrated in warm sterile water (≈35 °C) for 15 min before mixing.
  • Hydrate and pre-activate yeast, then mix thoroughly into the warm inoculant.

2. Tank preparation

  • Use a tank ≥250 L to provide working headspace.
  • Clean with hot water and a mild oxidizer (1% peroxide or dilute chlorine), then rinse thoroughly with clean water to remove residues.
  • Avoid rusty or greasy surfaces.

3. Cherry pre-treatment

  • Select ripe, undamaged cherries only.
  • Wash with clean water + 0.1% citric acid or light vinegar solution; rinse well.
  • Optional stronger treatment: quick spray/dip with 70% ethanol and immediate rinse with boiled & cooled water (field-adapted asepsis).
  • Goal: reduce surface load while maintaining fruit integrity.

4. Passive infiltration (thermal + immersion)

  1. Heat the inoculant to 38–40 °C in the tank (use shaded area and controlled heating). Do not exceed 42 °C to avoid thermal stress to the yeast.
  2. Fill the tank with the warm inoculant volume (200 L) and add cherries so they occupy ~60–70% of the tank volume.
  3. Ensure cherries are completely submerged; use a clean perforated weight or mesh to keep them under the surface.
  4. Allow the tank to cool slowly to ambient temperature (~24 °C). The cooling contraction of internal air spaces assists liquid infiltration.
  5. Keep cherries submerged for 24–48 h at 24–26 °C. Monitor for fine bubbling and a fruity aroma as signs of yeast activity.

5. Fermentation extension (optional)

After the initial infiltration step you can:

  • Remove the bulk liquid after 48 h if desired and keep cherries in a closed but not hermetic container to continue internal fermentation for another 24–36 h.
  • This step increases alcohol and fruity notes while limiting extended bacterial activity.

6. Basic monitoring & targets

  • Target pH start: 4.0–4.3; expected drop to 3.5–3.8 during fermentation.
  • Temperature: maintain 24–28 °C during active phase.
  • Signs of success: fine bubbling, clean fruity alcoholic aroma, absence of strong vinegar/putrid odors.

7. Sanitary considerations

  • Do not use rusty metal or containers with residues. Avoid chlorine residual in water.
  • Work with clean gloves and tools. Clean tank between batches and dry in sun where possible.
  • If contamination (foul odors, pellicle, excessive gas) appears, stop and sample for plating or discard safely.

8. Optional nutrient boost

To support rapid yeast growth, add ≈1 g/L melaza or cane juice (≈200 g for 200 L) to the inoculant before mixing. This provides nitrogen and micronutrients useful in field conditions.

9. Controls & sampling

  • Run three parallel mini-controls where possible: (A) natural (no sterilization, no inoculum), (B) sterilized surface, no inoculum (to observe recolonization), (C) sterilized + inoculum.
  • Sample liquid or pulp daily for pH, Brix (if available), and plating to confirm yeast dominance.

10. Limitations & notes

  • Intact skin prevents full, uniform infiltration — method increases penetration but does not guarantee 100% internal colonization.
  • Aggressive surface sterilization can damage the fruit and change fermentation dynamics.
  • Seal any injection points if you choose to inject some fruits to boost colonization; injection is labor-intensive at scale.
